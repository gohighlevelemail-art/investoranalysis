📈 Key Indices
?
Key Indices
The main scorecards of the US stock market. S&P 500 tracks 500 big companies. NASDAQ is tech-heavy. Dow Jones is 30 major companies. Russell 2000 tracks small companies.
Like checking the top scores in four different leagues. Each one tells you something different about the game.
S&P 500
?
S&P 500
500 of the largest US companies. The single most-watched number in global finance. If this is up, most things are up.
The report card for the 500 most popular kids in the school. If they're all doing well, school is doing well.
NASDAQ
?
NASDAQ
Tech-heavy index. Home to Apple, Google, Nvidia, Meta. When AI/tech is hot, NASDAQ outperforms. When rates rise, it often falls harder than the S&P.
The tech kids' table. When gadgets and apps are popular, this table is the loudest. When nerds fall out of fashion, it gets quiet fast.
DOW JONES
?
Dow Jones
Only 30 companies — older, more industrial. Boeing, Goldman, McDonald's. Less tech-forward. Good gauge of old-economy health.
The 30 oldest, most established students. Not the cool new kids, but the dependable ones that have been around forever.
RUSSELL 2000
?
Russell 2000
Tracks 2,000 small US companies. Considered a leading indicator — small caps react faster to economic conditions. If Russell is weak while S&P is strong, the rally may not be broad-based.
The junior varsity team. If they're losing while varsity is winning, it might mean the whole school isn't really doing great — just the star athletes.
VIX
?
VIX — Fear Index
Measures expected market volatility. Below 15 = calm. 15–25 = moderate concern. Above 30 = fear. Above 40 = panic. Higher VIX = more uncertainty in the market.
The school's stress level. If VIX is low, everyone's chill. If VIX is high, everyone's panicking about exams. You want to buy when everyone's panicking, sell when everyone's calm.
10YR TREASURY
?
10-Year Treasury Yield
The interest rate the US government pays to borrow money for 10 years. Rising yields = bad for growth stocks and real estate. It's the global benchmark for the 'risk-free rate.'
Imagine the safest possible investment — lending money to the US government. The rate they pay is the floor everything else is measured against. Higher floor = harder for risky bets to look attractive.
🏦 Fed & Macro Environment
?
Federal Reserve
The US central bank. They control interest rates. When they raise rates, borrowing costs go up and stocks often fall. When they cut, money flows easier and stocks often rise.
The Fed is like the school principal who sets the rules. When they tighten rules (raise rates), everyone slows down. When they loosen up (cut rates), people get more freedom — and more willing to take risks.
Fed Stance
?
Fed Stance
Is the Fed currently raising rates (hawkish), cutting rates (dovish), or holding steady? This sets the tone for the entire market.
Hawk = strict. Dove = relaxed. Hawk Fed = market gets nervous. Dove Fed = market gets excited.
Inflation Reading
?
Inflation (CPI)
How fast prices are rising. High inflation forces the Fed to raise rates. Low/falling inflation gives them room to cut. The target is around 2%.
If everything at the store costs more than last year, that's inflation. When it's too high, the Fed makes borrowing expensive to slow people down from spending.

?
Sector Rotation
Money moves between sectors depending on the macro environment. In a recession, defensive sectors (healthcare, utilities) do well. In a boom, cyclicals (tech, consumer, energy) lead. Spotting rotation early is an edge.
It's like weather in different parts of the country. While it's raining in one place, it's sunny somewhere else. Money moves to wherever the conditions are best right now.

Set Your Sentiment
?
Market Sentiment
Your gut read on whether investors are in risk-seeking mode (buying aggressive bets) or risk-avoiding mode (moving to safety). This affects how you size positions.
Is everyone at recess running around excitedly (Risk-On) or sitting quietly at their desks (Risk-Off)? The market has moods just like people do.
🎯 US Risk Gauge Checklist
?
Risk Gauge Checklist
Enter today's values for each indicator. The system applies fixed rules to classify each one as Risk-On, Neutral, or Risk-Off — then scores the overall market regime. No AI, just rules.
It's like a scorecard. Each indicator votes. The one with the most votes wins. If most things look scary, it's Risk-Off. If most things look good, it's Risk-On.
VIX Index
?
VIX — Volatility Index
Measures how much the market expects the S&P 500 to swing over the next 30 days. It's derived from options prices — when traders are scared and buying protection, VIX spikes. Often called the "Fear Index."
Imagine a weather forecast for storms in the stock market. VIX = 10 means clear skies. VIX = 40 means hurricane warning. The higher it is, the more scared everyone is.
Below 20 → calm, Risk-On  ·  20–30 → heightened uncertainty  ·  Above 30 → storm warning, Risk-Off

10yr Treasury Yield (level)
?
10-Year Treasury Yield — Day Change (bps)
Enter today's change in basis points (1 bps = 0.01%). When yields rise sharply, money shifts from stocks to bonds and borrowing gets more expensive — that's Risk-Off. When yields fall, the opposite happens.
Imagine the government's savings account interest rate. When that rate shoots up, everyone pulls money out of stocks to put in the "safe" account. That hurts stocks — Risk-Off.
Below 3.5% → Risk-On  ·  3.5–4.5% → Neutral  ·  Above 4.5% → Risk-Off
—
DXY US Dollar Index
?
DXY — US Dollar Index
Measures the US dollar's strength against a basket of 6 major currencies (Euro, Yen, Pound, etc.). A rising dollar makes US exports expensive and tightens financial conditions globally — generally Risk-Off for risk assets.
Imagine the dollar is a ruler everyone uses. When the ruler gets bigger (stronger dollar), everything priced in dollars costs more for foreigners. Less buying happens globally. That's usually bad for stocks.
Below 100 → weak dollar, Risk-On  ·  100–104 → Neutral  ·  Above 104 → strong dollar, Risk-Off
—
S&P 500 Day % Change
?
S&P 500 Daily Return
Enter today's percentage change in the S&P 500. A strong positive day signals risk appetite. A meaningful decline signals risk aversion. Small moves are noise.
If the S&P is up big today, people are buying — that's confident. If it's down big, people are selling and running scared. Small moves (less than 0.5%) don't tell you much.
Above +0.5% → Risk-On  ·  −0.5% to +0.5% → Neutral  ·  Below −0.5% → Risk-Off
—
IG Credit Spread (bps)
?
Investment Grade Credit Spread
The extra yield corporate bonds pay over US Treasuries. Tight spreads = lenders trust companies = Risk-On. Wide spreads = lenders scared of default risk = Risk-Off. Find this on FRED or Bloomberg as "ICE BofA IG OAS."
Imagine borrowing money from a friend. If they trust you, they lend at 2% extra. If they're scared you won't pay back, they charge 5% extra. Wide spread = scared lenders = bad sign for markets.
Below 90 bps → Risk-On  ·  90–130 bps → Neutral  ·  Above 130 bps → Risk-Off

European markets open before the US. Use this section to pick up early signals. ECB policy, energy prices, and geopolitics (Ukraine, Middle East) are the key drivers here.

📈 Key Indices
FTSE 100 (UK)
?
FTSE 100
The top 100 companies on the London Stock Exchange. Heavy in banking, oil (Shell, BP), and mining. Often moves with commodity prices and the pound sterling.
London's version of the S&P 500 but with more oil companies and banks. When oil is up, FTSE is usually happy.
DAX (Germany)
?
DAX
Germany's 40 largest companies. Very industrial and auto-heavy — BMW, Siemens, BASF. A proxy for European manufacturing health. Sensitive to China trade and energy prices.
Germany builds a lot of cars and machines. When factories are busy and trade is flowing, DAX is happy. When energy gets expensive (like in 2022), Germany hurts more than others.
CAC 40 (France)
?
CAC 40
France's top 40. Heavy in luxury goods — LVMH, Hermès, L'Oréal. Also has major defence (Airbus, Thales) and energy companies. A luxury goods bellwether.
France's stock market has a lot of luxury brands. When rich people worldwide are buying Gucci and Louis Vuitton, CAC does well.
EUR/USD
?
EUR/USD Exchange Rate
How many US dollars one Euro buys. Weak Euro makes European exports cheaper, helping exporters. Strong Euro hurts them but reduces import costs (like oil, which is priced in dollars).
If 1 Euro used to buy $1.20 but now only buys $1.05, the Euro got weaker. European companies that sell to the US love a weak Euro — their stuff becomes cheaper for Americans to buy.
Natural Gas (EU)
?
European Natural Gas
Europe imports most of its natural gas. Energy prices directly affect European manufacturing costs and inflation. Since the Russia-Ukraine war this has been a major macro factor for the region.
Europe needs to buy gas from other countries to heat homes and power factories. When gas prices spike, everything in Europe gets more expensive and companies suffer more.
ECB Rate
?
ECB — European Central Bank
Europe's version of the Federal Reserve. Sets interest rates for all Eurozone countries. ECB policy often lags the Fed — watch for divergence between the two as a trading signal.
Same as the Fed but for Europe. They control how cheap or expensive it is to borrow money in euros.
🗺 Geopolitical Flags
?
Geopolitical Risk
Wars, political instability, sanctions, and trade disputes that can move markets suddenly and unpredictably. Europe is closer to active conflict zones, making it more sensitive to geopolitical events.
When countries are fighting or imposing bans on each other, it disrupts trade and spooks investors. It's like when two families in the neighbourhood have a feud — it affects the whole street.

VSTOXX (European VIX)
?
VSTOXX — Euro Stoxx 50 Volatility
Europe's equivalent of the VIX. Measures expected volatility of the Euro Stoxx 50 index over 30 days. Typically moves with VIX but can diverge during Europe-specific crises (energy shocks, debt crises, elections).
Same as VIX but for Europe. If VSTOXX is high while US VIX is low, something is specifically scaring Europe — like an energy shortage or a big election result nobody expected.
Below 22 → calm, Risk-On  ·  22–32 → elevated fear  ·  Above 32 → high stress, Risk-Off

Euro Stoxx 50 Day %
?
Euro Stoxx 50 Daily Return
Europe's benchmark index of the 50 largest Eurozone companies. A strong day signals confidence in European growth. Because Europe opens before the US, this often sets the early tone for the New York open.
Europe's version of the S&P 500 scorecard. If Europe's school day started well, there's a good chance the US school day will too — they influence each other.
Above +0.5% → Risk-On  ·  −0.5% to +0.5% → Neutral  ·  Below −0.5% → Risk-Off

EUR/USD Day Change %
?
EUR/USD Daily Change
Enter today's % change in the Euro vs Dollar. Rising EUR/USD (Euro strengthening) suggests the dollar is weakening — typically Risk-On globally. Falling EUR/USD often signals dollar strength or euro-specific weakness — Risk-Off.
If the Euro is getting stronger vs the dollar today, it usually means investors are feeling brave and buying riskier things. If the dollar is getting stronger, people are running to safety.
Above +0.3% (Euro rising) → Risk-On  ·  −0.3% to +0.3% → Neutral  ·  Below −0.3% (Euro falling) → Risk-Off

German Bund 10yr Yield Change (bps)
?
German 10-Year Bund Yield — Day Change
Germany's 10yr government bond is Europe's equivalent of the US 10yr Treasury — the "risk-free" benchmark for the Eurozone. Rising Bund yields signal inflation/rate fears (Risk-Off for stocks). Falling yields signal a flight to safety or dovish ECB expectations.
Germany's government savings account rate. When it goes up fast, European investors get nervous about inflation and pull money from stocks. When it falls, people feel safer.
Below −5 bps → Risk-On  ·  −5 to +5 → Neutral  ·  Above +5 bps → Risk-Off

EU Natural Gas (TTF) Day %
?
TTF Natural Gas Price — Daily Change
TTF is the European natural gas benchmark (Title Transfer Facility, Netherlands). Europe imports most of its gas — when prices spike, manufacturing costs surge and inflation rises, squeezing corporate margins. A major Risk-Off signal specific to Europe.
Imagine gas prices for European factories. When gas gets expensive, it's like raising the price of electricity for every factory in Europe at once. Everything slows down and costs more — that's bad for stocks.
Below −2% (gas falling) → Risk-On  ·  −2% to +2% → Neutral  ·  Above +2% (gas spiking) → Risk-Off

Asia closes before North America opens. Overnight moves here — especially China — often set the early tone for US pre-market trading. China data is uniquely hard to trust; factor that into your read.

📈 Key Indices
NIKKEI 225 (Japan)
?
Nikkei 225
Japan's main index. Very sensitive to yen strength — a weak yen boosts Japanese exporters like Toyota and Sony. The Bank of Japan (BOJ) controls the yen through its interest rate policy.
Japan sells lots of cars and electronics worldwide. When the yen is cheap, Japanese products cost less for other countries to buy, so the companies make more money.
HANG SENG (HK)
?
Hang Seng
Hong Kong's stock exchange. Heavy in Chinese tech giants (Alibaba, Tencent) and Chinese banks. Sensitive to Beijing's regulatory decisions and US-China tensions.
Think of it as China's tech companies trading on a freer exchange. When Beijing announces new rules for tech companies, Hang Seng moves immediately.
Shanghai Composite
?
Shanghai Composite
Mainland China's main stock market. Often moves on PBOC (China's central bank) actions, government stimulus, and political signals. Less reliable as a free-market signal — the government actively manages it.
China's government sometimes steps in to support its stock market directly. It's less of a free market than the US. The news behind the number matters more than the number itself.
USD/JPY (Yen)
?
USD/JPY
How many yen one US dollar buys. A higher number = weaker yen. Watch closely — the BOJ's interest rate decisions cause massive yen moves that ripple into global markets.
Japan's currency is the yen. When it gets weaker vs the dollar, it's like everything in Japan goes on sale for foreigners — which is good for Japanese companies selling abroad.
PBOC / BOJ Action
?
PBOC & BOJ
People's Bank of China and Bank of Japan. Both have significant global impact — PBOC through stimulus and yuan management, BOJ through its unique ultra-low interest rate policy. Note any interventions here.
China and Japan's versions of the Fed. When they make big moves — like Japan raising rates for the first time in decades — global markets feel it immediately.
Commodities Signal
?
Commodities from Asia
China is the world's largest consumer of copper, iron ore, oil, and soybeans. Strong Chinese demand = higher commodity prices globally. Weak China = commodity prices fall.
China buys enormous amounts of raw materials. If China's economy slows down and they buy less copper, the price of copper drops for everyone in the world.

USD/JPY Day Change
?
USD/JPY — Yen as Safe Haven Signal
Enter today's change in USD/JPY (how many yen per dollar). When USD/JPY falls (yen strengthening), it means investors are buying yen as a safe haven — a Risk-Off signal. When USD/JPY rises (yen weakening), it often signals risk appetite returning.
The yen is like a global panic button. When things get scary worldwide, investors buy yen because Japan is considered safe. So if yen is getting stronger (USD/JPY number goes DOWN), people are scared somewhere in the world.

Copper Day % Change
?
Copper — Global Economic Barometer
Copper is used in construction, electronics, and manufacturing — everywhere in the economy. Because of this, traders call it "Dr. Copper" — it has a PhD in economics. Rising copper = global growth expected. Falling copper = slowdown feared.
Copper goes into almost everything made or built. If the price is going up, factories worldwide are busy and buying copper. That means the economy is growing — Risk-On. If copper is falling, factories are slowing down — Risk-Off.
Above +1% → growth signal, Risk-On  ·  −1% to +1% → Neutral  ·  Below −1% → slowdown signal, Risk-Off

Japan's benchmark of 225 large companies. Very sensitive to the yen — a weaker yen tends to boost the Nikkei because it makes Japanese exports cheaper. Also watches US tech heavily since many Japanese companies supply components to US tech firms.
Japan's school scorecard. Japan's companies love a cheap yen because their products get cheaper for Americans to buy. So often when the yen weakens, Nikkei goes up at the same time.

Hang Seng — China Proxy Signal
Hong Kong's index is the most liquid proxy for Chinese equities. Big moves here reflect Beijing policy changes, US-China tensions, or Chinese economic data. A strong Hang Seng often signals improving global risk appetite, especially for EM assets.
Think of Hang Seng as the report card for China's biggest companies. If it's up big, China's economy is probably doing well or the government just announced something positive. That's good for global markets.

China Manufacturing PMI
?
China PMI — Manufacturing Activity
Purchasing Managers Index for Chinese factories. Above 50 = expansion (factories getting busier). Below 50 = contraction (factories slowing down). Released monthly. Because China manufactures so much of the world's goods, this number moves global commodity prices and supply chains.
Imagine asking 1,000 Chinese factory managers: "Is business better or worse than last month?" PMI above 50 means more said better. Below 50 means more said worse. It's a monthly pulse check for the world's factory floor.
Above 51 → expansion, Risk-On  ·  49–51 → borderline  ·  Below 49 → contraction, Risk-Off

🔬 Deep Dive

A step-by-step research interview. Work through it before entering any position.

1. Identity
2. Business
3. Moat
4. Catalysts
5. Risks
6. Prediction
7. Score
What company or deal are you researching?
Start with the basics — name, ticker, what sector it's in, and which of your macro themes brought you here. If you can't connect it to a macro theme, question whether you should be looking at it at all.
Ticker Symbol
?
Ticker Symbol
The short code a stock uses on the exchange. Like a username for a company.
CVX = Chevron. AAPL = Apple. It's a nickname so traders can type fast.
Full Company Name
Sector
?
Sector
The industry category. Tech, Energy, Healthcare, Financials, etc. Sectors behave differently in different macro environments.
Like school subjects — Math, Science, English. Each one has different rules and each one does better or worse at different times.
Macro Theme Connection
?
Macro Theme Link
Which big-picture force does this company benefit from? Every position should connect back to a thesis. Otherwise it's just gambling.
If your big idea is "oil prices go up," then every oil company you pick should directly make more money when oil is expensive. You're checking the dots connect.
How does this company actually make money?
Be specific. Don't just say "they sell oil." Who are their customers? What product or service do they provide? What are their main revenue streams? How does this macro theme hit their income statement directly?
Business Model
?
Business Model
The specific mechanism by which a company earns revenue. Subscription? Transaction fees? Selling physical products? Licensing? Services?
Apple sells iPhones (products) AND charges monthly fees for iCloud (subscription). Two completely different ways to make money — one is lumpy, one is steady.
Revenue Drivers
?
Revenue Drivers
The specific factors that make the company's revenue go up or down. For an oil company: oil price and production volume. For a bank: interest rates and loan volume.
What makes the cash register ring more? For a lemonade stand: hot weather, a busy street, the price of lemons. Those are the revenue drivers.
Key Customers / Market
What protects this company from competition?
If this company is making great profits, why can't a competitor come in tomorrow and steal all their customers? The answer to that question is the moat. No moat = the profit eventually gets competed away.
Economic Moat
?
Economic Moat
A durable competitive advantage. Types: cost advantage (cheapest to produce), network effect (more users = more valuable), switching costs (hard to leave), intangible assets (patents, brand), efficient scale (natural monopoly).
A castle's moat stops enemies from getting in. A company's moat stops competitors from stealing customers. Warren Buffett invented this phrase. The wider the moat, the safer the profits.
Moat Type
What specific events could move this stock?
A good thesis has a trigger — a specific event that will force the market to reprice this stock in your direction. List them. Then set an expected timeline for each one.
Upcoming Catalysts
?
Catalyst
A specific, upcoming event that could cause the stock to re-rate — earnings, product launch, regulatory decision, court ruling, election, Fed meeting. The best trades have clear, near-term catalysts.
A catalyst is the spark. The firework (stock price) was already loaded. The catalyst is what lights the fuse. "Earnings on May 22" is a catalyst. "I think the company will do well eventually" is NOT a catalyst.
Nearest Catalyst Date
Time Horizon
?
Time Horizon
How long you plan to hold. Short-term trades need near-term catalysts. Long-term holds can be more thesis-driven. Mismatch between time horizon and catalyst is a common mistake.
Are you buying candy to eat today, or planting a tree you'll harvest in 5 years? The strategy is completely different.
What would prove you wrong?
This is the most important step. Before you put money in, decide what would force you to get out. Writing this now, before you have skin in the game, protects you from stubbornly holding a losing position.
Thesis Breakers
?
Thesis Breaker
Specific events or data that would invalidate your investment thesis. If oil prices fall 30% and you're long CVX for the oil thesis — that's a thesis breaker. Know it in advance.
Before betting your lunch money, decide: "If THIS happens, I'm out." Decide before the game, not during it. That stops you from being stubborn when you're losing.
Key Risks
What do you think happens, and why?
Write your prediction with a timestamp. In 6 months you'll look back at this and see if your logic was right — even if the outcome was wrong. This is how you train your instincts over time.
Your Prediction
?
Prediction Log
A dated record of your exact thinking. When the trade resolves, compare what you said to what happened. Right outcome for wrong reasons is as important to learn from as wrong outcomes.
Detectives write down their guesses before they solve the case. That way they can see if their instincts were good or bad. You're training your investment instincts here.
Price Target
?
Price Target
The price you expect the stock to reach if your thesis plays out. Not a guarantee — a reference point. If it hits your target, you evaluate whether to sell or hold.
You think this stock is worth $50. It currently trades at $35. Your price target is $50. If it gets there, you decide: take profits or let it run?
Project / Label Tag
?
Project Tag
Link this deep dive to one of your Projects. Keeps all your research organized by theme so you can come back to it anytime.
Like putting this worksheet in the right folder. So when you look for your "Oil Thesis" folder, this research is already in there.
How much do you believe in this idea?
Score your conviction from 1–10. This directly determines position sizing — high conviction gets bigger allocation. Be honest. A 6 should be sized differently than a 9.
Conviction Score (1–10)
?
Conviction Score
1 = speculative idea, allocate small. 5–6 = moderate confidence, medium size. 8–10 = high conviction, larger position. Never size a 4/10 idea the same as a 9/10 idea.
If you're 90% sure you'll win, bet more. If you're 50/50, bet small. Save your biggest bets for when you're most sure. Protect your chips.
Stage
Position Size
?
Position Sizing
What percentage of your total portfolio you're willing to allocate. High conviction ideas get more. Speculative ideas get less. Most professionals cap a single position at 5–15% of portfolio.
You have $100. How much do you put on this one bet? A 10/10 conviction might get $20. A 5/10 conviction gets $5. Never put all your eggs in one basket.

📖 Reference Guide

Every indicator explained. What it is, what it means, how to find it, and how to read the gauge.

🇺🇸 US Market Indicators
▼
These are the five indicators used in your US Risk Gauge. Fill them in at the start of every session before looking at any individual stock.
VIX Index ^VIX
The CBOE Volatility Index. Measures how much the market expects the S&P 500 to move over the next 30 days, derived from the prices of S&P 500 options. It doesn't tell you which direction markets go — only how violently they're expected to move. A low VIX means calm, expected small moves. A high VIX means traders are buying insurance against big swings.
It's a storm forecast for the stock market. VIX = 12 is clear sunny skies. VIX = 35 is a hurricane warning. It doesn't say if stocks go up or down — just how bumpy the ride will be. Smart investors watch it because big fear = potential buying opportunity, and too much calm = potential complacency before a drop.
Below 20 → Risk-On (calm, small daily moves, investors confident)  ·  20–30 → Neutral (heightened awareness, swings getting bigger)  ·  Above 30 → Risk-Off (storm warning — major fear, expect large daily swings)
Yahoo Finance: ^VIX CBOE.com investing.com → search VIX marketwatch.com
10yr Treasury Yield ^TNX
The annual interest rate the US government pays to borrow money for 10 years. It's the most important single number in global finance — the benchmark "risk-free" rate. Every other investment (stocks, real estate, corporate bonds) is priced relative to it. When this yield rises, money becomes more expensive everywhere and stocks face more competition from "safe" government bonds. Enter the level — not the daily change.
Imagine the government offers you a super safe savings account. If they pay you 4.5% per year, why would you take the risk of buying stocks that might lose money? You wouldn't. So when this rate goes up, stocks become less attractive — investors leave stocks for the "safe" government account. When the rate goes down, stocks look better again.
Below 3.5% → Risk-On (cheap borrowing, stocks competitive vs bonds)  ·  3.5–4.5% → Neutral  ·  Above 4.5% → Risk-Off (expensive money, bonds compete heavily with stocks)
Yahoo Finance: ^TNX CNBC.com → Markets → Bonds investing.com → Bonds → US 10Y fred.stlouisfed.org
DXY US Dollar Index DX-Y.NYB
Measures the US dollar's value against a basket of 6 major currencies — Euro (57.6%), Japanese Yen (13.6%), British Pound (11.9%), Canadian Dollar (9.1%), Swedish Krona (4.2%), Swiss Franc (3.6%). A rising DXY = stronger dollar = tighter global financial conditions. Most commodities are priced in dollars, so a strong dollar makes them more expensive for foreign buyers and can crush emerging markets. Enter the index level.
Imagine the US dollar is a measuring stick used worldwide. When the stick gets bigger (stronger dollar), everything priced in dollars — oil, gold, copper — costs more for people in other countries. So they buy less. That slows down global trade and usually hurts stocks outside the US. A weaker dollar is like a sale on global trade — everyone buys more.
Below 100 → Risk-On (weak dollar, easier global conditions)  ·  100–104 → Neutral  ·  Above 104 → Risk-Off (strong dollar tightening global liquidity)
Yahoo Finance: DX-Y.NYB investing.com → search US Dollar Index marketwatch.com → search DXY tradingeconomics.com
S&P 500 Day % Change ^GSPC
The daily percentage change of the S&P 500 — the benchmark index of 500 large US companies. Tracks $40+ trillion in market cap. Widely used as the single best gauge of US equity market health. Enter the day's percentage change (e.g. +1.2 or -0.8). This is the most direct signal of whether investors are in risk-seeking or risk-avoiding mode right now.
Think of the S&P 500 as the class average for the 500 most important companies in America. If the class average went up today, most people are doing well and feeling confident. If it went down, people are scared and selling. A tiny move (less than 0.5% either way) doesn't tell you much — it's just noise. Big moves tell you something real is happening.
Above +0.5% → Risk-On (buyers in control)  ·  −0.5% to +0.5% → Neutral (indecision, noise)  ·  Below −0.5% → Risk-Off (sellers in control)
Yahoo Finance: ^GSPC Google → search "S&P 500" cnbc.com → Markets wsj.com → Markets
IG Credit Spread FRED: BAMLC0A0CM
The extra yield (in basis points, where 100 bps = 1%) that investment-grade corporate bonds pay above US Treasuries. When companies need to pay more to borrow vs the government, it means lenders think there's more risk of not getting paid back. Tight spreads = confidence. Wide spreads = fear. This is one of the best early warning signals for a credit crunch or recession. Enter the current level in bps.
Imagine two people asking to borrow money. One is the US government (safest borrower on Earth). The other is a big company like Ford or AT&T. You'd charge the company more because there's more chance they can't pay back. The spread is how much MORE you charge them. When that extra charge gets big, it means even big companies are seen as risky — that's a scary sign for markets.
Below 90 bps → Risk-On (companies trusted, cheap to borrow)  ·  90–130 bps → Neutral  ·  Above 130 bps → Risk-Off (lenders scared, credit tightening)
fred.stlouisfed.org → search "ICE BofA IG OAS" Bloomberg Terminal (if available) wsj.com → Markets → Credit Markets
Fed Stance — Best Sources
The Federal Reserve's current posture — are they raising rates (hawkish), cutting (dovish), or holding? This sets the macro backdrop for everything. The Fed releases statements after every FOMC meeting (8 per year). Between meetings, watch Fed member speeches — they signal what's coming next.
The Fed is like the school principal who sets the rules for borrowing lunch money. Hawkish = strict, makes borrowing expensive. Dovish = relaxed, makes borrowing cheap. Everyone in the market watches which direction they're going next — it's the most important single variable for stocks and bonds.
federalreserve.gov → Press Releases CME FedWatch Tool (fedwatch.cmegroup.com) reuters.com → search "Fed" wsj.com → Economy section axios.com → Markets newsletter
Sector Check — Best Sources
Tracking which sectors are leading or lagging the market today. The 11 GICS sectors are: Technology, Healthcare, Financials, Consumer Discretionary, Consumer Staples, Energy, Industrials, Materials, Real Estate, Utilities, Communication Services. Sector rotation — money moving from one sector to another — often signals the next macro move before the index shows it.
The stock market is like a school with 11 different clubs. Sometimes the tech club is the most popular. Sometimes energy is. Money flows between these clubs depending on what's happening in the economy. If defensive clubs (utilities, healthcare) are getting popular, it means people are scared. If growth clubs (tech, consumer) are hot, people are confident.

🇪🇺 European Market Indicators
▼
European markets open 6 hours before North America. Use these indicators to get an early read on global risk appetite before the US opens.
VSTOXX ^V2TX
The Euro Stoxx 50 Volatility Index — Europe's equivalent of the VIX. Measures expected volatility of the Euro Stoxx 50 over the next 30 days. Structurally runs 2–4 points higher than VIX because European markets are less liquid and more exposed to geopolitical risk (energy crises, wars, political instability). Watch for divergence — when VSTOXX is high but VIX is low, something is specifically scaring Europe.
Same idea as VIX but for Europe's 50 biggest companies. Europe tends to be a bit more nervous than the US because they're closer to wars, depend more on imported energy, and have more complicated politics between countries. So a VSTOXX of 22 is actually pretty calm for Europe, while the same number on VIX would be starting to look elevated.
Below 22 → Risk-On (calm for European standards)  ·  22–32 → Neutral  ·  Above 32 → Risk-Off (significant European stress)
Yahoo Finance: ^V2TX investing.com → search VSTOXX stoxx.com
Euro Stoxx 50 ^STOXX50E
The benchmark index of Europe's 50 largest companies across 8 Eurozone countries. Heavily weighted toward: financials (banks like BNP, Santander), industrials (Siemens, Airbus), luxury (LVMH), and energy (TotalEnergies). Because Europe opens first, this index often gives you a preview of where the US will open — especially on major macro days. Enter the day's percentage change.
Europe's version of the S&P 500. It has 50 of the biggest companies in countries that use the Euro. Because Europe starts its school day 6 hours before the US, if something big happened overnight — like a central bank decision or a geopolitical event — you'll see the reaction here first, before the US market even opens.
Above +0.5% → Risk-On  ·  −0.5% to +0.5% → Neutral  ·  Below −0.5% → Risk-Off

EUR/USD Day % Change EURUSD=X
How much the Euro moved against the US dollar today, as a percentage. The most traded currency pair in the world — $1.8 trillion per day. When EUR/USD rises, the dollar is weakening, which is generally Risk-On (cheaper global borrowing costs, commodity prices up). When it falls, the dollar is strengthening — typically Risk-Off. Enter the day's % change, not the level.
One Euro used to buy you a certain amount of dollars. Today, did that change? If the Euro got stronger vs the dollar (EUR/USD went up), it usually means the dollar is getting weaker — which is like a sale on global risk. If EUR/USD went down (Euro weakened), the dollar is getting stronger — which usually means investors are nervous and running to the safety of the dollar.
Above +0.3% (Euro rising) → Risk-On  ·  −0.3% to +0.3% → Neutral  ·  Below −0.3% (Euro falling) → Risk-Off
Yahoo Finance: EURUSD=X xe.com investing.com → Currencies → EUR/USD
German Bund 10yr Yield Change ^BUND10Y
The daily change (in basis points) of Germany's 10-year government bond yield. Germany is the Eurozone's strongest economy, so its bonds are the European equivalent of US Treasuries — the "risk-free" benchmark for Europe. When Bund yields rise sharply, it signals inflation fears or ECB rate hike expectations — Risk-Off for European stocks. Enter the day's change in bps (e.g. +8 or -4).
Germany is the most trusted, financially stable country in Europe. So when Germany borrows money, the rate they pay is the safest rate in Europe. When that rate jumps up, it means investors expect higher inflation or the ECB to raise rates — both of which are bad for stocks in Europe. Same logic as the US 10yr, just for Europe.
Below −5 bps → Risk-On (yields falling, easier conditions)  ·  −5 to +5 bps → Neutral  ·  Above +5 bps → Risk-Off (yields rising, tighter conditions)
Yahoo Finance: ^BUND10Y investing.com → Bonds → Germany 10Y markets.ft.com → Government Bonds tradingeconomics.com → Germany → Government Bond Yield
TTF Natural Gas Day % TTF
The Title Transfer Facility (TTF) is Europe's benchmark natural gas price, traded in the Netherlands. Europe imports roughly 90% of its natural gas needs — making energy prices a uniquely powerful macro driver for European equities and inflation. Spiking TTF gas prices = rising manufacturing costs, higher inflation, pressure on ECB to hike = Risk-Off. Falling gas = relief for European industry = Risk-On.
Europe doesn't produce much of its own gas — it mostly buys it from Russia (historically), Norway, and the US. When gas prices spike, every factory, home, and office in Europe has to pay more. Companies make less profit. Governments have to subsidise energy. It's like if the price of school lunch suddenly doubled for every student — everything else in the school budget gets squeezed.
Below −2% → Risk-On (gas falling, relief for European industry)  ·  −2% to +2% → Neutral  ·  Above +2% → Risk-Off (gas spiking, pressure on European margins)
investing.com → Commodities → Natural Gas → TTF theice.com (ICE exchange) bloomberg.com → Energy → TTF tradingeconomics.com → Netherlands → Natural Gas
ECB — Best Sources
The European Central Bank sets interest rates for all 20 Eurozone countries. Its policy decisions lag the Fed by 6–12 months historically. Watch for divergence between ECB and Fed — when one is hiking while the other cuts, major currency moves happen. The ECB president (Christine Lagarde) speaks publicly after every policy meeting — those speeches move EUR/USD immediately.

🌏 Asian Market Indicators
▼
Asia closes before North America opens. These overnight moves set the early tone for pre-market trading. China data in particular moves global commodities.
USD/JPY Day Change JPY=X
How much USD/JPY moved today. The Japanese yen is one of the world's primary safe-haven currencies — when global risk appetite falls, investors rush into yen. A rising USD/JPY (yen weakening) often signals risk-on in Japan and Asia broadly. A falling USD/JPY (yen strengthening) signals safe-haven demand — investors are scared somewhere. Enter the day's change (e.g. +0.8 or -1.2).
Japan has been a financial safe haven for decades — the yen is like a global panic button. When investors get scared, they buy yen. When they buy yen, USD/JPY goes DOWN (yen gets stronger vs dollar). So: falling USD/JPY = fear somewhere in the world. Rising USD/JPY = investors are feeling brave and selling their yen to buy riskier things.
Rising (yen weakening) → Risk-On  ·  Small move → Neutral  ·  Falling (yen strengthening) → Risk-Off
Yahoo Finance: JPY=X xe.com → USD/JPY investing.com → Currencies → USD/JPY
Copper Day % Change HG=F
The daily % change in copper futures price. Copper is used in construction, electronics, electric vehicles, and virtually every manufactured good — making it the most economically sensitive commodity. Traders call it "Dr. Copper" because it has a PhD in economics — its price has historically predicted economic turning points better than most forecasters. Rising copper = global growth expectations rising. Falling copper = slowdown feared.
Copper goes into almost everything that gets built or made: houses, cars, phones, factories, power lines. If the price of copper is going up, it means factories worldwide are ordering more of it — business is good. If the price is falling, factories are ordering less — things are slowing down. It's like checking how busy the world's construction sites are just by looking at one number.
Above +1% → Risk-On (global growth expected)  ·  −1% to +1% → Neutral  ·  Below −1% → Risk-Off (slowdown signal)
Yahoo Finance: HG=F investing.com → Commodities → Copper tradingeconomics.com → Copper
Nikkei 225 Day % ^N225
Japan's benchmark index of 225 large companies. Unusually sensitive to the yen — a weaker yen directly boosts earnings of Japanese exporters (Toyota, Sony, Canon) because their overseas sales convert back to more yen. Also follows US tech closely since many Japanese companies supply components to US tech firms. Closes before US markets open, making it a useful overnight signal. Enter the day % change.
Japan sells enormous amounts of cars, electronics, and machinery to the rest of the world. When the yen is cheap, it's like all Japanese products go on sale for foreign buyers — so Japanese companies sell more and make more money. That's why when the yen weakens (USD/JPY goes up), the Nikkei often goes up at the same time. They move together.
Above +0.5% → Risk-On  ·  −0.5% to +0.5% → Neutral  ·  Below −0.5% → Risk-Off
Yahoo Finance: ^N225 investing.com → Indices → Japan → Nikkei nikkei.com (in English)
Hang Seng Day % ^HSI
Hong Kong's benchmark index — the most liquid proxy for Chinese equities accessible to global investors. Heavy in Chinese tech giants (Alibaba, Tencent, Meituan), Chinese banks, and property developers. Extremely sensitive to Beijing policy announcements, PBOC decisions, and US-China tensions. A strong Hang Seng day often signals improving global risk appetite for emerging market assets broadly.
China's biggest companies — like Alibaba (China's Amazon) and Tencent (China's Facebook + everything else) — trade in Hong Kong. When the Chinese government makes a new rule that helps tech companies, Hang Seng goes up. When there's a trade war with the US or a crackdown on businesses, it goes down. It's the fastest way to see what China's economy is doing.
Above +0.5% → Risk-On  ·  −0.5% to +0.5% → Neutral  ·  Below −0.5% → Risk-Off
Yahoo Finance: ^HSI investing.com → Indices → Hong Kong → Hang Seng scmp.com → Business (South China Morning Post)
China Manufacturing PMI Monthly release
The Purchasing Managers Index for Chinese manufacturing — a monthly survey of 500+ factory managers asking if business is better or worse than last month. Above 50 = expansion. Below 50 = contraction. There are two versions: the official NBS PMI (government, skews large state-owned enterprises) and the Caixin PMI (private sector, more market-driven). The Caixin version is generally more useful as an investment signal. Released on the 1st business day of each month.
Every month, someone calls 500 Chinese factory managers and asks: "Is your factory busier or slower than last month?" If more than half say busier, PMI is above 50 — China's factories are growing. If more say slower, it's below 50 — factories are shrinking. Because China is the world's manufacturing capital, when its factories slow down, the prices of copper, oil, and iron ore usually fall too.
Above 51 → Risk-On (expansion, global commodity demand rising)  ·  49–51 → Neutral  ·  Below 49 → Risk-Off (contraction, slowdown signal)
