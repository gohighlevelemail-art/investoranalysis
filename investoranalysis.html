📈 Key Indices
?
Key Indices
The main scorecards of the US stock market. S&P 500 tracks 500 big companies. NASDAQ is tech-heavy. Dow Jones is 30 major companies. Russell 2000 tracks small companies.
Like checking the top scores in four different leagues. Each one tells you something different about the game.
S&P 500
?
S&P 500
500 of the largest US companies. The single most-watched number in global finance. If this is up, most things are up.
The report card for the 500 most popular kids in the school. If they're all doing well, school is doing well.
NASDAQ
?
NASDAQ
Tech-heavy index. Home to Apple, Google, Nvidia, Meta. When AI/tech is hot, NASDAQ outperforms. When rates rise, it often falls harder than the S&P.
The tech kids' table. When gadgets and apps are popular, this table is the loudest. When nerds fall out of fashion, it gets quiet fast.
DOW JONES
?
Dow Jones
Only 30 companies — older, more industrial. Boeing, Goldman, McDonald's. Less tech-forward. Good gauge of old-economy health.
The 30 oldest, most established students. Not the cool new kids, but the dependable ones that have been around forever.
RUSSELL 2000
?
Russell 2000
Tracks 2,000 small US companies. Considered a leading indicator — small caps react faster to economic conditions. If Russell is weak while S&P is strong, the rally may not be broad-based.
The junior varsity team. If they're losing while varsity is winning, it might mean the whole school isn't really doing great — just the star athletes.
VIX
?
VIX — Fear Index
Measures expected market volatility. Below 15 = calm. 15–25 = moderate concern. Above 30 = fear. Above 40 = panic. Higher VIX = more uncertainty in the market.
The school's stress level. If VIX is low, everyone's chill. If VIX is high, everyone's panicking about exams. You want to buy when everyone's panicking, sell when everyone's calm.
10YR TREASURY
?
10-Year Treasury Yield
The interest rate the US government pays to borrow money for 10 years. Rising yields = bad for growth stocks and real estate. It's the global benchmark for the 'risk-free rate.'
Imagine the safest possible investment — lending money to the US government. The rate they pay is the floor everything else is measured against. Higher floor = harder for risky bets to look attractive.
🏦 Fed & Macro Environment
?
Federal Reserve
The US central bank. They control interest rates. When they raise rates, borrowing costs go up and stocks often fall. When they cut, money flows easier and stocks often rise.
The Fed is like the school principal who sets the rules. When they tighten rules (raise rates), everyone slows down. When they loosen up (cut rates), people get more freedom — and more willing to take risks.
Fed Stance
?
Fed Stance
Is the Fed currently raising rates (hawkish), cutting rates (dovish), or holding steady? This sets the tone for the entire market.
Hawk = strict. Dove = relaxed. Hawk Fed = market gets nervous. Dove Fed = market gets excited.
Inflation Reading
?
Inflation (CPI)
How fast prices are rising. High inflation forces the Fed to raise rates. Low/falling inflation gives them room to cut. The target is around 2%.
If everything at the store costs more than last year, that's inflation. When it's too high, the Fed makes borrowing expensive to slow people down from spending.

🎯 Sector Check
?
Sector Rotation
Money moves between sectors depending on the macro environment. In a recession, defensive sectors (healthcare, utilities) do well. In a boom, cyclicals (tech, consumer, energy) lead. Spotting rotation early is an edge.
It's like weather in different parts of the country. While it's raining in one place, it's sunny somewhere else. Money moves to wherever the conditions are best right now.

Set Your Sentiment
?
Market Sentiment
Your gut read on whether investors are in risk-seeking mode (buying aggressive bets) or risk-avoiding mode (moving to safety). This affects how you size positions.
Is everyone at recess running around excitedly (Risk-On) or sitting quietly at their desks (Risk-Off)? The market has moods just like people do.

European markets open before the US. Use this section to pick up early signals. ECB policy, energy prices, and geopolitics (Ukraine, Middle East) are the key drivers here.

📈 Key Indices
FTSE 100 (UK)
?
FTSE 100
The top 100 companies on the London Stock Exchange. Heavy in banking, oil (Shell, BP), and mining. Often moves with commodity prices and the pound sterling.
London's version of the S&P 500 but with more oil companies and banks. When oil is up, FTSE is usually happy.
DAX (Germany)
?
DAX
Germany's 40 largest companies. Very industrial and auto-heavy — BMW, Siemens, BASF. A proxy for European manufacturing health. Sensitive to China trade and energy prices.
Germany builds a lot of cars and machines. When factories are busy and trade is flowing, DAX is happy. When energy gets expensive (like in 2022), Germany hurts more than others.
CAC 40 (France)
?
CAC 40
France's top 40. Heavy in luxury goods — LVMH, Hermès, L'Oréal. Also has major defence (Airbus, Thales) and energy companies. A luxury goods bellwether.
France's stock market has a lot of luxury brands. When rich people worldwide are buying Gucci and Louis Vuitton, CAC does well.
EUR/USD
?
EUR/USD Exchange Rate
How many US dollars one Euro buys. Weak Euro makes European exports cheaper, helping exporters. Strong Euro hurts them but reduces import costs (like oil, which is priced in dollars).
If 1 Euro used to buy $1.20 but now only buys $1.05, the Euro got weaker. European companies that sell to the US love a weak Euro — their stuff becomes cheaper for Americans to buy.
Natural Gas (EU)
?
European Natural Gas
Europe imports most of its natural gas. Energy prices directly affect European manufacturing costs and inflation. Since the Russia-Ukraine war this has been a major macro factor for the region.
Europe needs to buy gas from other countries to heat homes and power factories. When gas prices spike, everything in Europe gets more expensive and companies suffer more.
ECB Rate
?
ECB — European Central Bank
Europe's version of the Federal Reserve. Sets interest rates for all Eurozone countries. ECB policy often lags the Fed — watch for divergence between the two as a trading signal.
Same as the Fed but for Europe. They control how cheap or expensive it is to borrow money in euros.
🗺 Geopolitical Flags
?
Geopolitical Risk
Wars, political instability, sanctions, and trade disputes that can move markets suddenly and unpredictably. Europe is closer to active conflict zones, making it more sensitive to geopolitical events.
When countries are fighting or imposing bans on each other, it disrupts trade and spooks investors. It's like when two families in the neighbourhood have a feud — it affects the whole street.
Sentiment

Asia closes before North America opens. Overnight moves here — especially China — often set the early tone for US pre-market trading. China data is uniquely hard to trust; factor that into your read.

📈 Key Indices
NIKKEI 225 (Japan)
?
Nikkei 225
Japan's main index. Very sensitive to yen strength — a weak yen boosts Japanese exporters like Toyota and Sony. The Bank of Japan (BOJ) controls the yen through its interest rate policy.
Japan sells lots of cars and electronics worldwide. When the yen is cheap, Japanese products cost less for other countries to buy, so the companies make more money.
HANG SENG (HK)
?
Hang Seng
Hong Kong's stock exchange. Heavy in Chinese tech giants (Alibaba, Tencent) and Chinese banks. Sensitive to Beijing's regulatory decisions and US-China tensions.
Think of it as China's tech companies trading on a freer exchange. When Beijing announces new rules for tech companies, Hang Seng moves immediately.
Shanghai Composite
?
Shanghai Composite
Mainland China's main stock market. Often moves on PBOC (China's central bank) actions, government stimulus, and political signals. Less reliable as a free-market signal — the government actively manages it.
China's government sometimes steps in to support its stock market directly. It's less of a free market than the US. The news behind the number matters more than the number itself.
USD/JPY (Yen)
?
USD/JPY
How many yen one US dollar buys. A higher number = weaker yen. Watch closely — the BOJ's interest rate decisions cause massive yen moves that ripple into global markets.
Japan's currency is the yen. When it gets weaker vs the dollar, it's like everything in Japan goes on sale for foreigners — which is good for Japanese companies selling abroad.
PBOC / BOJ Action
?
PBOC & BOJ
People's Bank of China and Bank of Japan. Both have significant global impact — PBOC through stimulus and yuan management, BOJ through its unique ultra-low interest rate policy. Note any interventions here.
China and Japan's versions of the Fed. When they make big moves — like Japan raising rates for the first time in decades — global markets feel it immediately.
Commodities Signal
?
Commodities from Asia
China is the world's largest consumer of copper, iron ore, oil, and soybeans. Strong Chinese demand = higher commodity prices globally. Weak China = commodity prices fall.
China buys enormous amounts of raw materials. If China's economy slows down and they buy less copper, the price of copper drops for everyone in the world.

🔬 Deep Dive

A step-by-step research interview. Work through it before entering any position.

1. Identity
2. Business
3. Moat
4. Catalysts
5. Risks
6. Prediction
7. Score
What company or deal are you researching?
Start with the basics — name, ticker, what sector it's in, and which of your macro themes brought you here. If you can't connect it to a macro theme, question whether you should be looking at it at all.
Ticker Symbol
?
Ticker Symbol
The short code a stock uses on the exchange. Like a username for a company.
CVX = Chevron. AAPL = Apple. It's a nickname so traders can type fast.
Full Company Name
Sector
?
Sector
The industry category. Tech, Energy, Healthcare, Financials, etc. Sectors behave differently in different macro environments.
Like school subjects — Math, Science, English. Each one has different rules and each one does better or worse at different times.
Macro Theme Connection
?
Macro Theme Link
Which big-picture force does this company benefit from? Every position should connect back to a thesis. Otherwise it's just gambling.
If your big idea is "oil prices go up," then every oil company you pick should directly make more money when oil is expensive. You're checking the dots connect.
How does this company actually make money?
Be specific. Don't just say "they sell oil." Who are their customers? What product or service do they provide? What are their main revenue streams? How does this macro theme hit their income statement directly?
Business Model
?
Business Model
The specific mechanism by which a company earns revenue. Subscription? Transaction fees? Selling physical products? Licensing? Services?
Apple sells iPhones (products) AND charges monthly fees for iCloud (subscription). Two completely different ways to make money — one is lumpy, one is steady.
Revenue Drivers
?
Revenue Drivers
The specific factors that make the company's revenue go up or down. For an oil company: oil price and production volume. For a bank: interest rates and loan volume.
What makes the cash register ring more? For a lemonade stand: hot weather, a busy street, the price of lemons. Those are the revenue drivers.
Key Customers / Market
What protects this company from competition?
If this company is making great profits, why can't a competitor come in tomorrow and steal all their customers? The answer to that question is the moat. No moat = the profit eventually gets competed away.
Economic Moat
?
Economic Moat
A durable competitive advantage. Types: cost advantage (cheapest to produce), network effect (more users = more valuable), switching costs (hard to leave), intangible assets (patents, brand), efficient scale (natural monopoly).
A castle's moat stops enemies from getting in. A company's moat stops competitors from stealing customers. Warren Buffett invented this phrase. The wider the moat, the safer the profits.
Moat Type
What specific events could move this stock?
A good thesis has a trigger — a specific event that will force the market to reprice this stock in your direction. List them. Then set an expected timeline for each one.
Upcoming Catalysts
?
Catalyst
A specific, upcoming event that could cause the stock to re-rate — earnings, product launch, regulatory decision, court ruling, election, Fed meeting. The best trades have clear, near-term catalysts.
A catalyst is the spark. The firework (stock price) was already loaded. The catalyst is what lights the fuse. "Earnings on May 22" is a catalyst. "I think the company will do well eventually" is NOT a catalyst.
Nearest Catalyst Date
Time Horizon
?
Time Horizon
How long you plan to hold. Short-term trades need near-term catalysts. Long-term holds can be more thesis-driven. Mismatch between time horizon and catalyst is a common mistake.
Are you buying candy to eat today, or planting a tree you'll harvest in 5 years? The strategy is completely different.
What would prove you wrong?
This is the most important step. Before you put money in, decide what would force you to get out. Writing this now, before you have skin in the game, protects you from stubbornly holding a losing position.
Thesis Breakers
?
Thesis Breaker
Specific events or data that would invalidate your investment thesis. If oil prices fall 30% and you're long CVX for the oil thesis — that's a thesis breaker. Know it in advance.
Before betting your lunch money, decide: "If THIS happens, I'm out." Decide before the game, not during it. That stops you from being stubborn when you're losing.
Key Risks
What do you think happens, and why?
Write your prediction with a timestamp. In 6 months you'll look back at this and see if your logic was right — even if the outcome was wrong. This is how you train your instincts over time.
Your Prediction
?
Prediction Log
A dated record of your exact thinking. When the trade resolves, compare what you said to what happened. Right outcome for wrong reasons is as important to learn from as wrong outcomes.
Detectives write down their guesses before they solve the case. That way they can see if their instincts were good or bad. You're training your investment instincts here.
Price Target
?
Price Target
The price you expect the stock to reach if your thesis plays out. Not a guarantee — a reference point. If it hits your target, you evaluate whether to sell or hold.
You think this stock is worth $50. It currently trades at $35. Your price target is $50. If it gets there, you decide: take profits or let it run?
Project / Label Tag
?
Project Tag
Link this deep dive to one of your Projects. Keeps all your research organized by theme so you can come back to it anytime.
Like putting this worksheet in the right folder. So when you look for your "Oil Thesis" folder, this research is already in there.
How much do you believe in this idea?
Score your conviction from 1–10. This directly determines position sizing — high conviction gets bigger allocation. Be honest. A 6 should be sized differently than a 9.
Conviction Score (1–10)
?
Conviction Score
1 = speculative idea, allocate small. 5–6 = moderate confidence, medium size. 8–10 = high conviction, larger position. Never size a 4/10 idea the same as a 9/10 idea.
If you're 90% sure you'll win, bet more. If you're 50/50, bet small. Save your biggest bets for when you're most sure. Protect your chips.
Stage
Position Size
?
Position Sizing
What percentage of your total portfolio you're willing to allocate. High conviction ideas get more. Speculative ideas get less. Most professionals cap a single position at 5–15% of portfolio.
You have $100. How much do you put on this one bet? A 10/10 conviction might get $20. A 5/10 conviction gets $5. Never put all your eggs in one basket.
